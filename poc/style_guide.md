# Future Weavers — Style Guide

**Target voice:** Kim Stanley Robinson meets Ted Chiang. Literary,
near-future speculative nonfiction-as-fiction. The narrator is a patient
historian looking back from an unspecified later vantage, sometimes wry,
never melodramatic.

## Do

- Ground speculation in **specific, named** things: a named researcher in
  Leipzig, a port in Chennai, a community kitchen in São Paulo. Concrete beats
  abstract every time.
- Give the year a **texture of daily life** — weather, food, what a screen
  shows, what people argue about on the bus — not just headlines.
- Let consequences compound. If 2028 had a grain crisis, 2029 still remembers
  it in the price of bread.
- Use occasional dry understatement. Something terrible may be summarised in
  one clause, then moved past, as history tends to.
- Prefer sentences of varied length. A long observational sentence can earn a
  short punch.
- Name recurring **narrative threads** (movements, projects, places) so later
  years can pick them up. If a thread already exists in state, honor it.

## Don't

- No hype words: "revolutionary," "unprecedented," "game-changing,"
  "transformative." Trust the events to do the lifting.
- No lists masquerading as prose. No bullet points.
- No present-tense sportscasting ("And now, the markets tremble!"). Calm past
  or narrative present.
- No real living private individuals by name. Heads of state / named public
  officials are OK in plausible policy roles. Tech CEOs: archetype only
  ("a Redmond software company"), never by name.
- No tidy morals. The year ends; it does not conclude.
- No US-centrism by default. Assume a global reader.

## Length & shape

- **400–600 words.** One to three sections separated by a blank line, no
  headings.
- Open on a specific scene or fact, not a thesis sentence.
- Close on an image or an unresolved tension, never a summary.
