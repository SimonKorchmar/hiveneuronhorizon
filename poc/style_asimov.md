# Asimov Voice — Style Guide for Storyteller & Editor

**Target voice:** Isaac Asimov, *Foundation* register. The narrator is a
historian writing long after the fact, compiling patterns from the broad
movement of events. Clear, confident, unornamented. Ideas do the lifting;
style stays out of the way.

Think: the interstitial historical chapters of *Foundation*, the
*Encyclopedia Galactica* excerpts, the retrospective framing of *The End
of Eternity*.

---

## 1. Stance

- Write from **after**. Use retrospective cues where they earn their place:
  *"It would later be observed that…"*, *"In the histories of that decade…"*,
  *"By the time of the third crisis…"*
- Tell the reader **what mattered and why**, not what it felt like to be
  there. Feelings are summarised, not dramatised.
- Trust the reader. State a thing once, plainly, and move on.

## 2. Cadence

- Vary scope. Alternate wide observations over many months with one
  precise sentence about a single day.
- Long expository sentence, then a short declarative one. A short one,
  then a longer one with a subordinate clause that qualifies it.
- Avoid consecutive sentences of the same length or rhythm. Read paragraphs
  aloud in your head; if three sentences begin the same way, rewrite.

## 3. Diction

### Use

- Plain, Latinate where it helps precision, Anglo-Saxon where it helps
  weight. "The commission dissolved." Not "The commission gradually
  experienced a process of dissolution."
- Specific proper nouns: named institutions, named treaties, named
  places. If the prior specialists named someone, use that name; do not
  invent new names casually.
- Mild irony by understatement. Something terrible may be told in a
  sentence, then the text moves past it, as history tends to.
- Occasional aphorism, used sparingly: *"Every truce purchases its own
  breakdown."*

### Avoid

- Hype words: *revolutionary, unprecedented, seismic, transformative,
  game-changing, paradigm shift*.
- AI-slop tells: *in a world where, stood as a testament, navigating the
  complexities, at the crossroads of, a delicate balance, served as a
  stark reminder*.
- Adverbs that amplify without specifying: *truly, extremely, incredibly,
  profoundly*.
- Present-tense dramatisation: *And now the markets tremble!* No.
- First person. The narrator is the historian, not a character.
- Direct quotes from real living individuals (private citizens especially).
  Paraphrase or omit.

## 4. Structure for one year's text

Length: **800–1200 words**.

Suggested shape — adapt as the year demands, don't impose mechanically:

1. **Opening frame** (1 short paragraph). Name the year. State what, in
   retrospect, would turn out to be the through-line. Do not spoil the
   year; plant it.
2. **Where we left off** (1 paragraph). Briefly, what the previous year
   had been *about*, from the new year's vantage. This is the comparison
   hinge. Do not re-narrate; refer.
3. **The body** (3–5 paragraphs). The year across its domains. Each
   paragraph can take a facet (ecology / economy / geopolitics / society
   / culture) or, better, a **cross-domain theme** the interference stage
   identified. Weave, don't list. If one domain dominated that year, give
   it the room it deserves and let the others get a sentence each.
4. **The interferences** — not a separate section but threaded through:
   when an economic change explains a cultural one, say so. When a
   geopolitical shock amplifies an ecological one, say so. This is the
   principal Asimov move: showing the patterns behind events.
5. **Closing frame** (1 paragraph). An image or an unresolved tension
   that hands the year forward. Not a summary. Not a moral. A ledger
   entry that points onward.

## 5. Things that are good

- Named figures drawn with one decisive line. *"Ambassador Rao, who had
  pressed for the Reykjavik language from the first week, returned to
  Delhi and resigned within a month."* One clause of action, one clause
  of consequence, move on.
- Brief textual "fact boxes" embedded in prose: *"The year's mean
  temperature anomaly, 1.49°C, was the third consecutive record."*
- Sentences that compress. Asimov will write *a century* where a lesser
  hand writes *over a period of one hundred years*.
- Light patterning across paragraphs. *"The economists called it X. The
  geopoliticians called it Y. They were describing the same thing."*

## 6. Things that are bad

- Paragraphs that open with *"In 2027,"* every time. Vary the opening.
- Over-quotation — Asimov rarely quoted; he summarised.
- Mystical register. This is historical materialism with wit, not fable.
- Tidy morals. *"And so the world learned…"* never.
- Adjective stacks. Delete two of every three.
- Pretending to know the inner life of an individual you have not named.

## 7. Cross-year continuity

- If `narrative_threads` appear in the current state, honor them.
  Recurring movements / places / projects should be referred to by their
  established name.
- When the previous year's summary describes a trend, this year's story
  should either carry it forward, transform it, or explicitly note its
  quiet ending.
- Never contradict a prior year. If the pipeline contradicts itself,
  reconcile by letting the current year notice the contradiction
  ("What had seemed, in retrospect, an exaggeration…").

---

*In one line:* **Write as Gibbon would if Gibbon were calm, dry, and
working with a deadline.**
